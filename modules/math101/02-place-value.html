Concept 1 · Place Value
What is Place Value?
The position of a digit determines its value.

In our number system (base 10), the position of a digit tells you how much it is worth. The same digit in a different position has a completely different value.

Place Value Columns Each position is 10× the one to its right:
Millions → Hundred Thousands → Ten Thousands → Thousands → Hundreds → Tens → Units
Then: Tenths → Hundredths → Thousandths
Example: 4,572
4 → Thousands = 4,000
5 → Hundreds = 500
7 → Tens = 70
2 → Units = 2
Total: 4,000 + 500 + 70 + 2 = 4,572
The digit 5 in 5,000 is worth 5,000. The digit 5 in 50 is worth only 50. Position matters!

Decimal Place Value
Digits after the decimal point also have place values.
After the Decimal Point Tenths (÷10): the first digit after the decimal
Hundredths (÷100): the second digit
Thousandths (÷1000): the third digit

Example: 3.47
3 → Units = 3
4 → Tenths = 0.4
7 → Hundredths = 0.07
0.057 — the 5 is in the hundredths column = 0.05, the 7 is thousandths = 0.007
Leading zeros after the decimal DO matter: 0.5 ≠ 0.05 ≠ 0.005
In the number 0.057, what is the value of the digit 5?

Rounding Numbers
Rounding gives an approximate value — easier to work with.
Rounding Rules 1. Identify the place you're rounding to
2. Look at the digit immediately to the RIGHT
3. If it's 5 or more: round UP
4. If it's 4 or less: round DOWN (keep the same)
5. Replace all digits to the right with zeros
Round 3,847 to nearest 100:
Hundreds digit = 8. Look right → 4 (less than 5). Round down.
Answer: 3,800

Round 3,867 to nearest 100:
Hundreds digit = 8. Look right → 6 (5 or more). Round up.
Answer: 3,900

Ordering & Comparing Numbers
Compare digit by digit, starting from the left.

To order numbers, always compare the highest place value column first. If those digits are equal, move to the next column.

Step-by-Step: Order 4,721 and 4,689 Thousands: both 4 — equal. Move right.
Hundreds: 7 vs 6 — 7 is larger.
So 4,721 > 4,689
Ordering decimals:
0.7 vs 0.69 — look at tenths: both 7 vs 6 → 0.7 > 0.69
Tip: add trailing zeros to help: 0.70 vs 0.69

Key Points to Remember
  • Each digit's position determines its value (ones, tens, hundreds, etc.)
  • Moving left multiplies value by 10; moving right divides by 10
  • Decimal columns: tenths (÷10), hundredths (÷100), thousandths (÷1000)
  • Rounding: look at the digit to the RIGHT of your target place
  • If that digit ≥ 5: round up. If < 5: round down (keep)
  • To compare numbers: start from the highest place value and work right
  • Trailing zeros in decimals don't change value: 0.5 = 0.50 = 0.500
