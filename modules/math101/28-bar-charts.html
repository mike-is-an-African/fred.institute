C6 · Bar Charts
What is a Bar Chart?

A bar chart uses rectangular bars to display the frequency (or value) of different categories. Each bar represents one category and its height shows the value.

Key Features Title: describe what the chart shows
X-axis (horizontal): categories
Y-axis (vertical): frequency/value with scale
Equal-width bars with equal gaps between them
Bars do NOT touch (unlike histograms!)
Always label BOTH axes. Always give the chart a title. The y-axis must start at 0 (or misleading results occur).

Reading & Interpreting Bar Charts
1
Read the title
Understand what the chart is measuring and for whom.
2
Read the axes labels and scale
X-axis: categories. Y-axis: what's being measured. Note the scale intervals.
3
Read individual bar heights
Trace across from the top of each bar to the y-axis to find the value.
4
Make comparisons
Difference = taller bar value − shorter bar value. Range = highest − lowest.
In a bar chart: Mon=45, Tue=30, Wed=55, Thu=25, Fri=60. What is the range of values?

Compound & Dual Bar Charts
Dual Bar ChartShows two sets of data side by side for each category. Uses two different colours. Needs a key/legend.
Compound (Stacked) Bar ChartBars are stacked — each bar shows multiple categories divided by colour. Good for showing totals AND proportions at once.
A dual bar chart shows boys and girls for 4 sports. What does the key/legend tell you?
C6 · Bar Charts
Bar Chart vs Histogram
Bar ChartFor discrete/categorical data. Bars have gaps between them. Width of bars doesn't matter — only height.
HistogramFor continuous grouped data. No gaps between bars. Area of bar (not height) = frequency. Uses frequency density on y-axis.
Key distinction: bar chart uses discrete/categorical data and has gaps. Histogram uses continuous data and has NO gaps.
