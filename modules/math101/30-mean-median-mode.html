Mean, Median, Mode & Range
Four measures that describe a data set.
MeanAdd all values, divide by how many there are.
Mean = (sum of values) ÷ (number of values)

Data: 3, 5, 7, 5, 10. Mean = 30÷5 = 6
MedianThe MIDDLE value when data is arranged in ORDER.
Even number of values: average the two middle values.

Data: 3, 5, 5, 7, 10. Median = 5
ModeThe value that appears MOST OFTEN.
There can be more than one mode (bimodal) or no mode.

Data: 3, 5, 5, 7, 10. Mode = 5 (appears twice)
RangeMaximum value − minimum value. Measures spread (not an average).
Data: 3, 5, 5, 7, 10. Range = 10 − 3 = 7

Finding the Median — Step by Step
Odd number of values Step 1: Arrange in order
Step 2: Find middle position: (n+1)/2
Step 3: That value is the median

Data: 8, 3, 5, 9, 1 → Ordered: 1, 3, 5, 8, 9
n=5. Position=(5+1)/2=3rd. Median = 5
Even number of values Average the two middle values.

Data: 4, 8, 2, 6 → Ordered: 2, 4, 6, 8
n=4. Middles: 4th/2=2nd and 3rd → 4 and 6
Median = (4+6)/2 = 5

Choosing the Right Average
When to use MeanWhen data is fairly spread and there are no extreme outliers. Most common in everyday use. Affected by outliers.
When to use MedianWhen data has outliers (extreme values) that would skew the mean. E.g. salaries: one very high earner would inflate the mean.
When to use ModeFor categorical data, or when you need the most common value. E.g. most popular shoe size in a shop.

C6 · Averages
Key Points
  • Mean = sum ÷ count. Affected by outliers.
  • Median = middle value (ordered). Not affected by outliers.
  • Mode = most frequent. Can have none or multiple.
  • Range = max − min. Measures spread, not average.
  • From frequency table: mean = Σ(f×x) ÷ Σf
  • Use median for skewed data, mode for categories, mean for symmetric data.
