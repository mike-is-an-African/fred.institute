What is a Verb?
A verb is a word that expresses an action, state, or occurrence.

Verbs are the engine of every sentence. Without a verb, a sentence cannot exist. A verb tells us what the subject does, is, or has. Every complete sentence must have at least one verb.

Types of VerbsAction verbs: show physical or mental action — run, think, write, eat
Linking verbs: connect the subject to a description — is, am, are, was, were, become, seem
Helping (auxiliary) verbs: support the main verb — have, has, had, do, does, did, will, shall, can, could, may, might
Modal verbs: show possibility, permission, obligation — can, could, may, might, must, should, would
Action: She runs every morning.
Linking: He is a teacher.
Helping: They have been waiting.
Modal: You should study harder.

Verb Tenses — Overview
Verbs change form to show when something happens.
Three Main Time FramesPast: already happened — walked, ran, ate
Present: happening now or regularly — walk, run, eat
Future: will happen — will walk, will run, will eat
Simple vs Continuous vs PerfectSimple: I eat (habit) | I ate (completed) | I will eat
Continuous: I am eating (now) | I was eating (then) | I will be eating
Perfect: I have eaten (completed with present relevance) | I had eaten | I will have eaten
The form of the verb changes to match the tense. Regular verbs add -ed for past. Irregular verbs change completely: go→went, eat→ate, see→saw.

Regular vs Irregular Verbs
How verbs change in the past tense.
Regular VerbsAdd -ed to form the past: walk→walked, play→played, cook→cooked
Spelling rules: double the consonant after a short vowel (stop→stopped), change y to i (carry→carried)
Common Irregular VerbsThese must be memorised — no pattern:
go→went→gone | see→saw→seen | take→took→taken
eat→ate→eaten | write→wrote→written | speak→spoke→spoken
come→came→come | run→ran→run | begin→began→begun
The three forms: Base → Past Simple → Past Participle
Regular: play→played→played (same for both)
Irregular: eat→ate→eaten (all different)

Subject-Verb Agreement
The verb must agree with its subject in number.
RulesSingular subject → singular verb: The dog runs. He is tired.
Plural subject → plural verb: The dogs run. They are tired.

Tricky cases:
• "Everyone" is singular: Everyone is welcome (NOT are)
• "The team" is singular: The team has won (NOT have)
• Two subjects joined by "and" = plural: Tom and Jerry are friends
Quick test: if you can replace the subject with "he/she/it", use singular verb. If you can replace with "they", use plural.

Active vs Passive Voice
Who does the action — and does it matter?
Active VoiceThe subject DOES the action:
"The cat caught the mouse." (subject = cat, does the catching)
Passive VoiceThe subject RECEIVES the action:
"The mouse was caught by the cat." (subject = mouse, receives the catching)
Active is usually preferred — clearer and more direct.
Passive is used when the action matters more than the doer, or when the doer is unknown:
"The window was broken." (We don't know who broke it.)
